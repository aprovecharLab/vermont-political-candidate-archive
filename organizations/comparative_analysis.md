# Comparative Analysis: Vermont Progressive Party vs. DSA, SDA, SI, and SDUSA

Below is a side-by-side comparison across key dimensions. One important upfront clarification: "SDA" refers here to the modern **Social Democrats of America**, a 501(c)(4) incorporated in 2021 in Missouri, which operates at socialists.us. It is distinct from the historic **Social Democracy of America** (1897), which was an organization led by Eugene Debs that split into the Social Democratic Party of America and later fed into the Socialist Party of America.

## Detailed Comparison by Theme

### 1. Ideological Orientation

The **VPP** platform (as amended November 9, 2025) is considerably more explicitly socialist than commonly perceived. While the party is often described in media as simply "progressive," the platform itself calls for "an economy based on collective ownership, democratic decision-making, and shared prosperity," the creation of a Vermont State Bank to "wrestle control over investment from the financial sector to the people," the bringing of "critical infrastructure and services, such as electrical utilities and broadband, under public ownership," and the creation of "publicly owned companies as democratic alternatives to for-profit corporations." The preamble envisions a society "where society's wealth is no longer concentrated in the hands of a few, but shared equitably among the people who are the source of that wealth." This places the VPP firmly in the **democratic socialist** tradition—not merely progressive or social democratic—distinguished by its explicit embrace of collective ownership and its anti-capitalist framing, while remaining grounded in practical, state-level governance rather than revolutionary rhetoric.

The **DSA** identifies as a **democratic socialist** organization. It calls for social control of the means of production, the abolition of capitalist exploitation, and the end of systemic oppression along lines of race, gender, sexuality, and class. It has evolved from its 1982 founding (which had a pro-Zionist orientation under Michael Harrington) to an explicitly **anti-Zionist** stance, which distinguishes it from the other groups on this list.

The **SDA** positions itself as aligned with the **Socialist International's 1989 Stockholm declaration**. It seeks to educate Americans—particularly Democrats—about socialism as distinguished from communism and anarchism. It is reformist and electoral in orientation.

The **SI** is an international umbrella body whose members span the spectrum from moderate social democrats (like many European parties) to more avowedly socialist organizations. It advocates for **democratic socialism** achieved through reformist, parliamentary means rather than revolution.

The **SDUSA** historically occupied the **right wing of American socialism**—social democratic, anti-communist, pro-labor, and oriented toward working within the Democratic Party. It was a direct successor to the Socialist Party of America (the party of Eugene Debs and Norman Thomas). By the 2020s, it is essentially dormant.

### 2. Electoral Strategy and Real-World Impact

The **VPP** is unique on this list as a genuine **third party that wins elections**. It holds major-party status in Vermont, runs its own candidates on its own ballot line, and has consistently elected state legislators and city council members. This makes it arguably the most electorally successful left-of-Democratic-Party organization in the United States, despite its small geographic footprint. Notably, the platform also calls for **ranked choice voting** and **proportional representation**—reforms designed to break the two-party duopoly, which is consistent with the party's existence as an independent electoral vehicle.

The **DSA** does not run as a party but instead endorses candidates—primarily in Democratic primaries—and has had notable success. DSA-affiliated politicians including Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, Rashida Tlaib, Jamaal Bowman, and Cori Bush have been elected to Congress. With 216 elected officials nationwide and 120,000+ members, DSA is the **largest socialist organization in U.S. history**.

The **SDA** is primarily an educational and advocacy organization. It does not appear to run or endorse candidates in a structured way and is much smaller in scale. It explicitly frames itself as operating *within* the Democratic Party.

The **SI** has no direct electoral role in the U.S. but serves as an international coordinating body. Its member parties govern or are major opposition forces in numerous countries (e.g., Labour parties in Europe, the UK, etc.).

The **SDUSA** was once an influential voice in American social democratic politics—its members were involved in the AFL-CIO and civil rights movement—but it has not been politically active for years.

### 3. Relationship to the Democratic Party

This is one of the starkest dividing lines:

- **VPP**: Fully independent. Runs its own candidates. Sometimes its legislators caucus with Democrats, but the party is organizationally distinct. The platform's call for proportional representation and ranked choice voting reflects a commitment to breaking the two-party system entirely.
- **DSA**: Ambivalent. Officially an independent organization, but strategically engages with the Democratic Party through primary endorsements. There is ongoing internal debate about whether to form an independent party.
- **SDA**: Explicitly sees itself as the "socialist faction inside the Democratic Party."
- **SI**: Not applicable (international body).
- **SDUSA**: Historically favored working within the Democratic Party—a position that contributed to its decline as the DSA emerged as a more dynamic alternative.

### 4. Foreign Policy

The VPP platform's "War and Militarism" section reveals a **clearly articulated anti-imperialist foreign policy stance** that was not reflected in my initial analysis. The platform explicitly:

- Opposes "foreign wars and America's intervention in the affairs in other nations"
- Calls for "ending the military industrial complex and reducing spending on the military"
- Supports "the cancellation of sovereign debt so that each nation can fund programs that uplift its people"
- Pledges to "actively campaign to end apartheid, mass murder, and genocide, as well [as] the complicity of American governments and corporations in such actions"
- Supports "local efforts to show solidarity with exploited people worldwide"
- Declares in its preamble opposition to "war, genocide, and colonialism"

This positions the VPP closer to DSA on foreign policy than previously indicated—both are anti-imperialist and anti-militarist. However, the VPP's language is somewhat less specific than DSA's on particular conflicts (e.g., Israel/Palestine), using broader terms like "genocide" and "colonialism" without naming specific countries. The preamble's reference to "colonialism" and the platform's call to "condemn the complicity of American governments and corporations" in marginalization suggests solidarity with global anti-colonial movements.

By comparison:

- **DSA**: Has taken stronger, more specific foreign-policy stances—explicitly anti-Zionist, critical of NATO, sympathetic to certain Palestinian "resistance" narratives. This has drawn criticism from organizations like the ADL and AJC.
- **SDA**: Aligns with the Socialist International's internationalist framework and explicitly seeks to distinguish democratic socialism from communism.
- **SI**: Takes positions on global issues including disarmament, climate change, decolonization, and human rights. Has addressed Israel-Palestine through the BDS lens, drawing some controversy.
- **SDUSA**: Historically pro-NATO, anti-communist, and supportive of U.S. Cold War alliances—a posture that put it at odds with the New Left and places it at the opposite end of the spectrum from both VPP and DSA on foreign policy.

### 5. Public Ownership and Economic Structure

The VPP platform's "Public Ownership" section is one of the most striking findings from the actual document. The party explicitly calls for:

- "An economy based on collective ownership, democratic decision-making, and shared prosperity"
- Worker-owned cooperatives and "other models of collective ownership"
- Bringing "electrical utilities and broadband" under public ownership
- "Publicly owned companies as democratic alternatives to for-profit corporations"
- A Vermont State Bank to "wrestle control over investment from the financial sector to the people"
- "Publicly-owned, ecologically sustainable trash collection, recycling, waste hauling, and landfills"

This level of specificity on public ownership places the VPP **closer to DSA** on economic structure than the "progressive" label typically implies. Both organizations advocate for collective ownership and public control of key industries, though the VPP articulates these demands in the context of a specific state government while DSA frames them nationally.

The **SDA** supports similar principles (universal healthcare, UBI, pro-union policies) but its public-facing material focuses more on education and values than on specific public-ownership demands.

The **SI** supports "public ownership or democratic control of key economic resources" as a general principle but leaves specifics to member parties.

The **SDUSA** historically supported a mixed economy with a strong welfare state and labor movement, but did not emphasize public ownership to the degree that the VPP platform does.

### 6. Taxation and Redistribution

The VPP platform contains one of the most detailed redistributive tax agendas of any organization on this list, including:

- A state-level **wealth tax on millionaires**
- A **financial transactions tax** on stock trades and derivatives
- A **capital gains tax** on profits from stocks or property, with higher rates for higher earners
- An **inheritance tax** on large estates
- Higher taxes on second homes and rental properties
- A more progressive state income tax with a higher top marginal rate

While DSA broadly supports wealth redistribution, the VPP's platform is notable for its **granular, state-level specificity**—these are concrete legislative proposals, not aspirational national slogans.

### 7. Housing

The VPP's housing platform is remarkably detailed and radical:

- Housing treated as "a fundamental human right"
- Eliminating zoning laws that prevent dense, affordable housing
- **Constructing publicly owned housing developments**
- A statewide rental registry and **rent control laws**
- Statewide **just cause eviction** protections
- **Free legal counsel** for tenants in housing court
- Restricting short-term rentals

This goes beyond what DSA, SDA, SI, or SDUSA have publicly articulated at this level of policy specificity, reflecting the VPP's grounding in actual governance and legislative drafting.

### 8. Criminal Justice

The VPP platform includes a decarceral agenda that places it alongside DSA:

- Ending the use of **private and out-of-state prisons**
- Shifting from incarceration toward "recovery, education, and training in a non-traumatic environment"
- Investing in social work, mental health, and substance abuse treatment as alternatives to "hostile policing"
- Fair sentencing and bail laws

This aligns closely with DSA's "abolish private prisons" position, though the VPP frames it more as reform of existing systems rather than abolition.

### 9. Education

The VPP platform calls for:

- Tuition-free education at Vermont's public universities, colleges, and vocational schools
- Fully funded public education through progressive taxation
- Opposition to "all forms of privatization, charter schools, and for-profit education providers"
- A transformative curriculum teaching "labor history, Black liberation, Indigenous resistance, anti-colonial struggle, and the role of class conflict in shaping our world"
- Free breakfast and lunch for all public school students
- Expanded public pre-K, childcare, and after-school programs

The inclusion of "class conflict" and "anti-colonial struggle" in the curriculum proposal is a notably explicit Marxist-adjacent framing for a governing political party, and places the VPP closer to DSA's ideological orientation than is commonly understood.

## Historical Lineage

The organizations have tangled genealogies:

- The **Social Democracy of America** (1897) → split into the **Social Democratic Party** (1898) → merged into the **Socialist Party of America** (1901) → eventually renamed **Socialist Party, U.S.A.** (1964) → renamed **Social Democrats, USA** (1972) → largely dormant today.
- The **DSA** was founded in 1982 from the merger of the Democratic Socialist Organizing Committee (DSOC, led by Michael Harrington, himself a former Socialist Party member) and the New American Movement (NAM). Many SDUSA members eventually migrated to DSA.
- The **modern SDA** (2021) claims lineage from the Socialist International's values but has no direct institutional continuity with the 1897 organization of the same initials.
- The **VPP** has roots in Burlington's 1980s progressive movement, notably associated with Bernie Sanders' tenure as mayor of Burlington (1981–1989), though the party is formally independent of Sanders. The 2025 platform—particularly its public ownership, anti-imperialist, and anti-capitalist sections—suggests the party has moved further left over time, or perhaps that its ideology was always more explicitly socialist than external observers credited.
- The **SI** traces back to the Second International (1889) and was formally re-established in 1951.

## Revised Summary Assessment

| Criterion | Most Notable |
|---|---|
| **Electoral success** | VPP (wins under own ballot line) and DSA (large-scale primary endorsements) |
| **Membership scale** | DSA (~120,000+) |
| **Longevity / institutional depth** | SI (since 1951, 100+ member parties globally) |
| **Historical significance** | SDUSA (direct successor to the Socialist Party of America) |
| **Pragmatic local governance with radical platform** | VPP (the only one governing at a municipal/state level *while* calling for public ownership, a state bank, and anti-imperialism) |
| **Ideological clarity** | DSA (explicitly anti-capitalist, democratic socialist) — though the VPP platform now shows comparable ideological depth |
| **Most detailed policy platform** | VPP (the 2025 platform is extraordinarily specific on taxation, housing, public ownership, and criminal justice) |
| **Current relevance** | DSA and VPP are the only two with active, meaningful political operations today |

In essence, the **VPP and DSA** are the two organizations on this list with real, ongoing political impact in the United States—and the VPP's 2025 platform reveals they are closer together ideologically than commonly portrayed. The VPP is not merely a "progressive" or "populist left" party; its platform explicitly calls for collective ownership, public ownership of key industries, a state bank, anti-imperialism, rent control, a wealth tax, and a curriculum teaching class conflict and anti-colonial struggle. What distinguishes the VPP from DSA is not ideology so much as **strategy and scale**: the VPP operates as an independent party in one state with detailed, implementable policy proposals, while DSA operates nationally as a mass membership organization endorsing candidates within the Democratic Party primary system.

The **SDA** is a small, newer organization trying to carve a niche as the socialist wing of the Democratic Party. The **SI** remains globally significant but has limited direct U.S. relevance. And **SDUSA**, while historically important, is essentially a dormant relic of an earlier era of American social democracy—one whose pro-NATO, anti-communist orientation stands in stark contrast to the anti-imperialist platforms of both the VPP and DSA today.

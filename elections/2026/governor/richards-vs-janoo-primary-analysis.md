# Vermont Gubernatorial Democratic Primary, 2026: A Comparative Analysis of Aly Richards and Amanda Janoo

## Introduction

The August 11, 2026 Democratic primary for Governor of Vermont presents voters with a choice between two candidates who, while sharing broad progressive goals, diverge meaningfully in ideology, approach, and depth of alignment with social democracy political traditions. With incumbent Republican Governor Phil Scott seeking re-election amid declining approval ratings, the Democratic nominee will enter the general election with a competitive advantage in one of the most left-leaning states in the country. Understanding the distinctions between Aly Richards and Amanda Janoo — their backgrounds, policy platforms, and relationships to broader political movements — is essential for voters, organizers, and political observers ahead of the primary.

This analysis draws on campaign websites, news coverage, candidate forums, and social media communications to provide a comprehensive side-by-side comparison. Since Democratic Socialism is in the news nationwide, this comparison further situates each candidate's platform relative to four significant democratic socialist political organizations: the Democratic Socialists of America (DSA), Social Democrats of America (SDA), the Socialist International (SI), and Social Democrats USA (SDUSA).

## Candidate Backgrounds

### Aly Richards

Aly Richards is a resident of Montpelier and the mother of twin boys. She has never held elected office but served as deputy chief of staff to former Governor Peter Shumlin and chairs the University of Vermont Medical Center board. Her most prominent credential is her decade as CEO of Let's Grow Kids, a statewide advocacy organization that mobilized over 40,000 Vermonters to pass Act 76 in 2023 — Vermont's landmark child-care law, which created a payroll tax to fund income-based child-care subsidies for families. Richards announced her candidacy on April 6, 2026, at a campaign launch in her hometown of Newbury, framing the state's challenges as a "five-alarm fire" and arguing that the strategies used to pass the child-care law can be applied to housing, healthcare, and education. She emphasizes her "deep understanding about the machinery of how the state of Vermont works" and her track record of moving legislation "to actually get a result that's going to work for Vermonters."

### Amanda Janoo

Amanda Janoo was born and raised in Strafford, Vermont, and now rents in Burlington. She attended Macalester College, became a Fulbright researcher, and studied development economics at the University of Cambridge before working with governments, international institutions, and the United Nations in various countries. She founded the Wellbeing Economy Alliance of Vermont (WEAV) and has served on the boards of Rights & Democracy and the ACLU Vermont, as well as on the Steering Committee of the Fund Vermont's Future Initiative. Janoo announced her candidacy on March 10, 2026, outside the Strafford Town House, becoming the first Democrat to challenge Governor Phil Scott. She frames her campaign around a "community wealth" and "wellbeing economy" vision, arguing that "the systems themselves are the problem" and that Vermont can "illustrate that a different economic and political system is possible."

## Platform Comparison

### Core Philosophy

Richards positions herself as a practical legislator-advocate who has demonstrated results within existing state systems. Her central argument is that the organizing and legislative strategy that succeeded in child care can be replicated to address housing, healthcare, and education. Her platform is solutions-oriented and sectoral: identify the problem, build a coalition, pass legislation, deliver results. She speaks of "a new approach" but defines that approach in terms of proven methods applied to new sectors.

Janoo offers a more systemic critique. Her platform is built on the premise that "the government's job is to ensure every Vermonter has the necessary foundations to live a life of dignity and purpose." She explicitly challenges "trickle-down economic logic" and argues for building an economy "where wealth stays local, work is rewarded, communities are resilient, and Vermont's future is shaped by the people who live here — not by distant corporations or investors." Her approach is not merely sectoral reform but economic restructuring through community ownership, participatory governance, and local wealth-building.

### Housing

Both candidates propose increasing taxes on second homes to generate revenue for housing programs and keep properties available to Vermont residents. Richards frames housing within her broader economic action plan, calling for a strategic vacancy tax, a comprehensive look at how vacancy affects housing supply, and building "more homes, in the right places, at prices Vermonters can actually afford." She links housing to workforce shortages — Vermont "can't solve its housing crisis without the workforce to build the housing we need."

Janoo's approach to housing is embedded in her community-wealth framework. She proposes keeping homes in Vermonters' hands, strengthening local economies, and protecting communities from speculative investment. Her proposed Office of Community Wealth Building and Rural Economic Development would coordinate housing policy alongside other community-wealth initiatives, connecting local supply with local demand.

### Healthcare

Richards proposes expanding healthcare access using the funding and coalition-building model that succeeded in child care. She treats healthcare as one of several sectors where her proven approach can deliver progress. Her platform includes supporting Propositions 3 and 4 on the November 2026 ballot, which would codify reproductive freedom and collective bargaining rights in the Vermont Constitution.

Janoo explicitly calls for "free healthcare" and "universal, affordable health care" as a foundational right. Her platform frames healthcare as a matter of dignity and purpose, arguing for "tackling root causes rather than symptoms." She has also opposed Governor Scott's education reform efforts, arguing that they fail to address healthcare costs, which she identifies as a primary cost driver in the state budget.

### Education

Richards supports broader education improvements and affordability measures, though specific education policy proposals are less detailed in available materials. She frames education alongside housing and healthcare as interconnected challenges requiring coordinated state action.

Janoo explicitly opposes forced school consolidation, a key component of Governor Scott's education reform agenda. She argues that school consolidation fails to address the underlying cost drivers and undermines local democratic control. Her platform emphasizes strengthening Town Meeting and participatory governance as alternatives to top-down consolidation.

### Economy and Labor

Richards proposes a "Vermont Economic Action Plan" — a coordinated strategy to make Vermont a place where people can afford to stay, work, and raise families. She emphasizes recruiting nurses, teachers, and workers across sectors and frames economic development as inseparable from housing and workforce development.

Janoo's economic platform is her most distinctive feature. She proposes a Vermont Office of Community Wealth Building and Rural Economic Development — a dedicated, cross-agency office that would coordinate community-wealth policies across state government, serve as a one-stop resource for towns and businesses, connect local supply with local demand, and help scale successful local ownership models statewide. She envisions Vermont as "a national leader in the next generation of American manufacturing — clean, durable, circular, and community-rooted." Janoo has also been endorsed by three major unions: the Vermont-NEA, VSEA, and CIR/SEIU Healthcare — all three unions that have weighed in on the race.

### Climate Change and Environmental Policy

Richards frames climate as one of several urgent "five-alarm fire" challenges. She has met with the Vermont Youth Lobby at their Rally for the Planet and pledges to weave climate action into her broader economic agenda through clean-energy development, natural resource protection, and climate-resilient infrastructure. Her specific proposals include systematically replacing culverts, hardening infrastructure against flooding, and building the kind of resilience that keeps "houses out of rivers and families in their homes." She treats energy independence and climate resilience as intertwined, framing climate preparedness as a practical necessity for Vermont's physical and economic future.

Janoo takes a more expansive view. She states plainly: "I will always prioritize climate and ecological action." Her platform positions climate not as one issue among many but as inseparable from economic transformation. She calls for energy self-sufficiency, expanded renewable power, and a manufacturing transition that creates good jobs while reducing emissions. Her vision positions Vermont as a national leader in clean, durable, circular, community-rooted manufacturing — proving that "climate action and economic prosperity can go hand in hand." For Janoo, climate is not merely a resilience challenge but a catalyst for systemic economic restructuring.

### Reproductive Rights

Both candidates are strongly pro-choice and explicitly supportive of Proposition 3 on the November 2026 ballot, which would codify abortion access and reproductive freedom in the Vermont Constitution.

Richards' platform states directly: "Reproductive freedom, collective bargaining, equal protection — these are not negotiable. As Governor, I will defend every right Vermonters have secured and fight back against any federal attempt to take them away."

Janoo frames reproductive freedom within her broader platform of ensuring every Vermonter has "the necessary foundations to live a life of dignity and purpose." She has served on the board of Rights & Democracy, a progressive advocacy organization that works on reproductive rights among other issues. Both candidates approach reproductive rights as settled policy to be defended rather than debated, and both emphasize resistance to potential federal rollback efforts.

### Immigration

This is the area of sharpest divergence between the two candidates.

Richards acknowledges that "the federal government's immigration enforcement actions are inflicting fear in Vermont communities and among our neighbors, who are woven into the fabric of our state." She pledges to be "a transparent leader who ensures people know their rights" and to "bring relevant partners together to share accurate information and prepare for an unknown future." Her approach is defensive and protective — ensuring Vermonters know their rights, resisting federal overreach through legal and community channels, and positioning Vermont as "a beacon of hope for the country."

Janoo takes a significantly more activist and confrontational stance. Her campaign materials include slogans such as "ICE OUT OF VT!" and "BRING ICE TO JUSTICE." She has participated in protests against ICE and supported legislation to limit or ban federal immigration officer activities in Vermont. She has stated that "only abolishing ICE + Border Patrol can truly prevent" harm to immigrant communities — a position that goes substantially further than protective rights-defense. Janoo has been visibly present at protests following a chaotic encounter between ICE agents and demonstrators in South Burlington, and she has worked in coalition on legislative efforts to protect Vermont's immigrant community.

## Alignment with Social Democracy Political Organizations

This analysis examines policy overlaps, not formal endorsements or memberships. Neither candidate appears to have declared formal affiliation with any of these organizations. All four are assessed based on their published platforms and public statements as of July 2026.

### Democratic Socialists of America (DSA)

The DSA's 2025-2026 program, "Workers Deserve More," centers on building "thriving working-class communities" through universal childcare, Medicare for All, free public college, a comprehensive Green New Deal, robust labor union empowerment, a living minimum wage, expanded paid family leave, universal child care, voting rights protection, opposition to imperialist wars, support for Palestinian self-determination, a new democratic constitution with proportional representation, and the end of money in politics.

Richards shows moderate alignment with DSA priorities. Her central achievement — universal child-care funding through Act 76 — directly overlaps with DSA's universal childcare demand. Her support for progressive taxation, housing programs, healthcare expansion, reproductive rights, and climate resilience all intersect with DSA policy goals. However, she does not frame her platform in class-struggle language, does not use Green New Deal terminology explicitly, has limited visible engagement with labor unions relative to Janoo, and does not address foreign policy or systemic constitutional reform — though the latter two are largely outside the scope of a state gubernatorial race.

Janoo demonstrates stronger alignment across the board. Her universal healthcare language is explicit. She has earned endorsements from all three major unions that weighed in on the race (NEA, VSEA, SEIU Healthcare). Her climate platform ties job creation to emissions reduction and manufacturing transition, echoing Green New Deal logic. Her community-wealth framework resonates with DSA's anti-corporate, pro-worker economic vision. Her call to abolish ICE directly mirrors DSA's stated position. Her emphasis on participatory democracy and strengthening Town Meeting aligns with DSA's commitment to democratic reform. She does not, however, explicitly use the term "socialist" or formally align with DSA, and her platform operates within the existing framework of Vermont's government and economy rather than calling for its fundamental replacement.

### Social Democrats of America (SDA)

The Social Democrats of America (socialists.us) is a socialist organization affiliated with the Socialist International. Its platform advocates for free education, universal healthcare, anti-death penalty policies, reproductive rights (pro-abortion), universal basic income (UBI), the right to unionize, separation of church and state, and the belief that government should make life bearable for all and ensure citizens' liberties. It positions itself as part of the Democratic Party's Socialist Caucus and maintains connections to the broader international socialist movement.

Richards shows moderate alignment with SDA priorities. Her universal childcare achievement, healthcare expansion goals, reproductive rights defense, and progressive taxation approach all intersect with SDA's core demands. However, she does not address UBI, does not frame her platform in explicitly socialist terms, and does not emphasize separation of church and state as a campaign issue. Her pragmatic, sectoral approach is compatible with SDA's reformist instincts but lacks the organization's more explicit socialist self-identification.

Janoo demonstrates stronger alignment with SDA. Her universal healthcare stance, labor union endorsements, reproductive rights support, and community-wealth economic vision all overlap with SDA's platform. Her systemic economic critique — challenging "trickle-down economic logic" and envisioning "a different economic and political system" — comes closer to SDA's socialist framing than Richards' technocratic approach. However, Janoo does not explicitly address UBI or separation of church and state, and her platform operates within the Democratic Party rather than positioning itself as part of a distinct socialist caucus.

### Socialist International (SI)

The Socialist International's Declaration of Principles affirms a commitment to democratic socialism grounded in universal human rights, gender equality, climate justice, social solidarity, and an internationally coordinated economy placed under democratic control to reduce poverty and inequality. It calls for advanced participatory democracy guaranteeing political, social, and economic freedoms; public ownership and democratic management of key productive resources; universal access to education, healthcare, and housing; workers' rights to organize; and a foreign policy based on peace, anti-imperialism, and solidarity among peoples.

Richards shows moderate alignment with SI principles. Her expansion of public services through democratic processes, defense of constitutional rights, and climate resilience efforts intersect with SI priorities. However, she does not emphasize participatory democracy as a distinct pillar, does not address public ownership, and does not frame her economic vision in terms of democratic control of productive resources.

Janoo demonstrates stronger alignment with SI's declaration. Her emphasis on participatory governance — strengthening Town Meeting, deepening democracy, and proposing a Future Generations Act — directly echoes SI's call for "advanced, participatory democracy." Her community-wealth framework, with its focus on resident-owned businesses and local ownership models, partially aligns with SI's support for "social ownership and democratic control of productive resources." Her international development background (UN, Cambridge, Fulbright) provides a perspective consistent with SI's global solidarity framework. Her climate justice framing connects environmental action to economic justice in ways SI explicitly endorses. Her immigration stance — rooted in human rights for all people and resistance to federal overreach — aligns with SI's human-rights-centered approach to migration.

### Social Democrats USA (SDUSA)

SDUSA promotes a reformist, pro-labor agenda within a mixed-economy framework: universal publicly funded healthcare and education, a living-wage federal minimum wage, progressive taxation, expanded Social Security, affordable housing, strong collective-bargaining rights, climate action through a Green New Deal-type program, abolition of the death penalty, protection of reproductive rights, full LGBTQ and civil-rights equality, separation of church and state, and a foreign policy rooted in multilateralism, human rights, and continued NATO commitment.

Both candidates align reasonably well with SDUSA's reformist approach. Richards' sectoral reform strategy — taking a proven legislative model and applying it to new sectors — is quintessentially social democratic in its method. Her work within existing state institutions to expand public services is consistent with SDUSA's incrementalist philosophy. Her support for progressive taxation (second-home taxes), affordable housing, universal childcare, healthcare expansion, and reproductive rights all fit comfortably within the SDUSA framework.

Janoo's mixed-economy community-wealth model — which keeps market mechanisms while redirecting wealth to local communities — is also broadly compatible with social democracy. Her union endorsements, universal healthcare stance, and climate-economy integration align well. However, her call for abolishing ICE exceeds SDUSA's reformist immigration framework, which supports humane enforcement and immigrant rights but does not call for dismantling federal immigration agencies. Janoo's more systemic economic critique also strains slightly against SDUSA's explicitly pro-NATO, multilateralist, and reformist posture, though the difference is one of emphasis rather than fundamental contradiction.

## Key Observations and Takeaways

**1. Neither candidate identifies formally as socialist.** Both run as Democrats on pragmatic, mainstream progressive platforms. Neither has claimed formal affiliation with DSA, SDA, SI, or SDUSA. However, Janoo's rhetoric and policy framework come considerably closer to explicitly socialist language than Richards', particularly in her systemic critique of "trickle-down economic logic" and her vision of demonstrating "that a different economic and political system is possible."

**2. Janoo consistently shows stronger alignment with all four organizations.** This is driven by several factors: her explicit universal healthcare language, her three major labor union endorsements (NEA, VSEA, SEIU Healthcare), her community-wealth framing that echoes socialist economic critique, her call for abolishing ICE (directly mirroring DSA's position), her emphasis on participatory democracy and strengthening Town Meeting (resonating with SI's and SDA's principles), her climate-economy integration (echoing Green New Deal logic), and her international development background (consistent with SI's and SDA's global solidarity frameworks).

**3. Richards takes a more technocratic, sectoral approach.** Her strategy of replicating a proven legislative model — build a coalition, identify funding, pass a law, deliver results — is effective and demonstrably successful, as Act 76 shows. This approach is compatible with social democratic reformism (particularly SDUSA's and SDA's reformist instincts) but does not adopt socialist ideological framing. She emphasizes "the machinery of how the state of Vermont works" rather than questioning whether the machinery itself needs fundamental change. This makes her positions broadly compatible with SDUSA's and SDA's reformist frameworks while showing more limited alignment with DSA's and SI's more transformative visions.

**4. The sharpest divergence between the candidates is on immigration.** Richards takes a defensive, protective, rights-education approach — ensuring Vermonters know their rights and resisting federal overreach through legal and community channels. Janoo takes an abolitionist, confrontational approach — calling for the dismantling of ICE and Border Patrol, participating directly in protests, and supporting legislation to ban federal immigration activities in Vermont. This is the area where Janoo most clearly aligns with DSA's maximalist position and where she exceeds the reformist frameworks of both SDUSA and SDA.

**5. On reproductive rights, both candidates are strongly aligned** with each other and with all four organizations. This is a settled issue within the Democratic primary field. Both explicitly support Proposition 3 codifying reproductive freedom in the Vermont Constitution and both frame reproductive rights as non-negotiable. DSA, SDA, SI, and SDUSA all explicitly protect reproductive rights in their platforms.

**6. On climate, Janoo again goes further** — explicitly tying climate action to manufacturing transformation, job creation, and systemic economic change. Richards focuses more on infrastructure resilience and clean energy development within existing frameworks. Janoo's approach more closely mirrors the Green New Deal logic shared by DSA, SDUSA, and SI, while Richards' approach is more practically oriented toward immediate adaptation and resilience. SDA's platform does not detail specific climate proposals beyond general environmental commitments, but its sister organization SI's climate justice framing aligns more closely with Janoo's approach.

**7. Context matters.** These are Vermont state-level candidates, not national office seekers. Their platforms necessarily focus on state-jurisdiction issues — education, healthcare access, housing, infrastructure, economic development — rather than foreign policy, national constitutional reform, or international coordination. The absence of foreign policy positions from both candidates' platforms should not be interpreted as disinterest but as a reflection of the office they seek. This limits meaningful comparison with the foreign policy planks of DSA, SDUSA, SI, and SDUSA.

**8. Fundraising is competitive.** As of the July 1, 2026 filing deadline, both candidates reported substantial fundraising hauls that exceeded Governor Scott's $48,000 in contributions (though Scott retains a $218,000 cash surplus). Both Democrats have demonstrated the ability to mount credible general election campaigns.
